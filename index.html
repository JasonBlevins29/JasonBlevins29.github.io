Analyzing the 2016 US Presidential Election

After the suprising result of the 2016 US presidential election, many people were left dumbfounded, seekng answers to how President Trump got elected. Many asked: Who voted for him and why? The following series of visualizations to seeks to find some correlations and patterns between election results and public demographic data.

What prompted me to choose this data was the commonly seen red and blue maps. This map in paticular stood out to me as the number of counites that President Trump won was great in proportion to Hillary Clinton. I noticed that the few counties that Hillary Clinton won were near large metropoliton areas and I created the following visualization to answer the question: "Is the population density of a county correlated their tendency to vote for one Candidate or another?".

Percent Republican Vote Against Population Denisty Per County

Hover over dots for more information on that county.

Judging by the scatterplot above, this seems to be the case. It is apparant that the less dense counties, clustered at the top left of the graph voted in a high percentage for President Trump.

One explination for this trend is that the small rural counties have been negatively affected economically and have been left behind in the service and technology based economy of the 21st century economy. In order to verify if this notion was correct, I decided to plot the unemploymentRate since 1950 for the top counties that voted for President Trump and Hillary Clinton over time. The data was gathered from the US Census.

Unemployment of Most Lopsided Counties Over Time

Republican counties in red. Democrat counties in blue. Black line is national average.

This graph was dificult to understand, so I created another one that was similar, but instead of plotting absolute unemployment, I plotted the resepctive unemployment of the counties as it relates to the national average. In this graph, when the line for a specific county is below the black line, the unemployment is below the national average for that specific year.

Adjusted Unemployment of Most Lopsided Counties Over Time.

There did not seem to be a clear trend based on this chart. What I did notice that seven out of the ten most lopsided counties had higher unemployment than average. This made me wonder: "Does high unemployment drive counties to vote extreme one way or another?".

In order to answer this question, I made a quick scatter plot with the y axis being unemployment rate and the x axis being percent voted for President Trump. If the answer to the previous question is yes, we would expect to see a U shape with high unemployment clustered near the outsides and low in the middle. This was not the case.

Unemployment Against Percent GOP Vote.

Republican counties in red. Democrat counties in blue. Black line is national average.

The trend that seemed to appear was that as the percentage of republican vote increased, unemployment decreased. This became my next qeuestion. In order to answer this question, I averaged the unemployment rate for counties, grouped into deciles.

Unemployment for decile voted for President Trump.

This visualization confirms the idea that counties with a higher percentage voted for Trump, have lower unemployment. This is somewhat inconsistent with the belief that I had that Trump had gained large support in ares that had high unemployment and that had been left behind in the economic recovery following the 2008 recession. In fact, the highest unemployment rate is correlated with counties where Trump only recieved 10-20 percent of the vote.

This series of visualiztions both confirms and denies some paticular beliefes that I, and likely others, held about voter demographics. I found scatter plots very useful in viewing a very large amount of data and finding trends for further analysis. The scatter plot seems to preserve the most data from a data set, as long as the number of variables is limited to two. However, as in the case of the second scatter plot, a trend is not as noticeable as it is in a simplier visualiztion such as a bar chart. By splitting the data that was represented in the scatter plot into catagories, a clear trend can be shown. These visualizations as a whole show that different techniques are appropriate for showing different relationships and different data sets.

The data I used for these visualizations was a combination of datasets from the United States Censuses, dating back from 1950 to 2010, United States Census 5-year community estimates and election data that was from townhall.com and scraped and compiled by Tony McGovern.
